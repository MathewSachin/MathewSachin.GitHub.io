*Just because something can be done in DevTools doesn’t mean it should be published as a tutorial. Capability is technical; responsibility is social.*

Over time, I found I needed a clearer boundary for what belongs on this blog and what doesn’t.

## The soft-wall / hard-wall distinction

The most useful framing is simple:

- **soft wall**: client-side presentation barriers (overlays, hidden elements, disabled controls)
- **hard wall**: server-side authorization, authentication, entitlement, or access controls

Explaining how client-side UI behaves can be educational. Publishing operational bypass guidance for hard controls is not something I consider responsible.

## A publication filter that scales

Before writing any browser-hack content, I evaluate two questions:

1. Is there genuine educational value?
2. What is the realistic abuse potential?

If educational value is high and abuse potential is low, publication can make sense with clear context. If abuse potential is high, the right path is usually private disclosure or heavily abstracted discussion without executable misuse detail.

## Why intent and framing matter

Two posts can use similar primitives and have very different real-world outcomes depending on framing.

A post focused on user-side customization, accessibility, and debugging can empower users safely. A post framed as a bypass playbook can encourage misuse even if the underlying API calls look “basic.”

That means editorial choices—scope, language, omitted details—are part of security posture.

## What I avoid publishing

I avoid publishing step-by-step material that materially lowers effort for:

- unauthorized access to private data
- account compromise workflows
- meaningful security-control evasion
- abuse-at-scale automation

You can still teach defensive thinking and browser internals without enabling misuse.

## Responsible disclosure over “viral speed”

If something looks like a genuine vulnerability, responsible disclosure is slower but better: verify carefully, report privately, give maintainers time, then publish sanitized lessons if appropriate.

It is less dramatic than shipping a “hack” thread immediately, but better for users and ecosystems.

## The standard I try to meet

If I can’t explain why a post helps users more than it helps attackers, it probably shouldn’t ship in its current form.
